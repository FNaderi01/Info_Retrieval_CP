                                Modern-day Mark Twain joins governor raceTerri Hallenbeck
| Free Press Staff Writer
Much of the hoopla surrounding Thursday's candidate filing deadline focused on Republican Scott Milne's unorthodox last-minute decision that he would indeed run, but from the top to the bottom of the ballot, there were other points of interest:• A three-person GOP primary for governor: While Milne is the candidate that has many in the Vermont Republican Party breathing a sigh of relief that the party will have some semblance of a candidate, Steve Berry of Wolcott also slid into the race. Berry and Milne join Emily Peyton of Putney for a three-person Republican primary.Berry declared on his petition that he is the "modern-day Mark Twain." (He looks like the famed author, he said.)Berry, a retired marketer, said he collected signatures on his petition in the last week before the deadline because he was afraid no one would challenge incumbent Democrat Peter Shumlin. He said he plans to challenge Milne and Peyton to debates in every county ahead of the Aug. 26 primary."I see this state heading in the wrong direction," said Berry, a 72-year-old grandfather of 19 who said he and his wife were ousted from their Duxbury home by Tropical Storm Irene.He argued that the lack of competition under government-run health care will yield long waiting lists that the Veterans Administration is under fire for having. He said he would also take on the new Common Core education requirements from the federal government.To recap, others running for governor: Libertarian Dan Feliciano, Liberty Union candidate Peter Diamondstone, and independents Cris Ericson and Bernard Peters.• Welch opposition: Rep. Peter Welch, who has won re-election easily every time since his initial 2006 tough campaign for the U.S. House against Republican Martha Rainville, has a total of three Republicans vying for the chance to face him this year.Mark Donka, a Woodstock police officer who lost 72 to 23 percent against Welch in 2012, is back. So are Donald Russell and Donald Nolte, who both say they have no political experience.Nolte, of Newport, says on his website that he is a 13-year Vermont resident who worked in the computer programming field. He argues that federal spending is out of control and supports a flat tax.Russell, of Shelburne, says he is an Army veteran and is semi-retired from the U.S. Immigration and Naturalization Service. He supports gun rights, English as the official national language and a flat tax. He says he supports health coverage for all but opposes the federal Affordable Care Act. His website includes some of his poetry.Donka, of Hartford, describes himself as a conservative who believes in smaller government, less taxation and more freedom. He supports repealing the federal health care law.• Shumlin/Sorrell primaries: Never mind the talk about who will be Gov. Peter Shumlin's Republican opponent, he also has a Democratic one, who happens to be a lifelong Republican. H. Brooke Paige, said he opposes Shumlin's plans for a single-payer, government-funded health coverage system, which he describes as "Shumlin's war on doctors and their patients."Paige is also running in the Democratic primary for attorney general against incumbent Bill Sorrell. He argues that Sorrell "has not really been home as directing the activities of the state police and the Attorney General's Office on keeping drugs under control."• The Prog candidate for treasurer: Progressive Don Schramm is still running for treasurer though his name is absent from the list of those who submitted petitions by Thursday's deadline. The party's state committee has nominated Schramm, and the party will encourage supporters to write him in on the primary ballot, Vice Chairman Morgan Daybell said.
